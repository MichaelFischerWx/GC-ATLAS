The Atmospheric General Circulation Atlas

See the base state
of Earth's atmosphere.

An interactive ERA5 reanalysis on a spinnable globe — 1961 through the present, at every level, field, and season.

Hadley cells, jet streams, stationary waves, storm tracks, the Walker circulation — the bones of the atmospheric general circulation, rendered live. Spin the globe, pick a month, choose a year or a 30-year climatology, toggle the eddies off, slice a cross-section, seed a parcel and watch it ride the climatological flow. For students building intuition, researchers probing a hypothesis, educators pulling a figure, and anyone curious about how the atmosphere works.

Why this tool

A foundation, made visible.

The atmospheric general circulation has historically been explored through textbook diagrams and static figures. GC-ATLAS replaces those with a live, observed, spinnable reference — the real ERA5 base state, at every level, every field, every month from 1961 through last week.

The atmospheric general circulation is famously hard to visualise. The concepts are three-dimensional, span the globe, evolve with the seasons, and can only be fully appreciated as decompositions — zonal mean versus eddy, stationary versus transient, rotational versus divergent. A static figure captures one slice of one of those decompositions, at one season, at one level. The real atmosphere is all of them at once.

GC-ATLAS makes every one of those slices one click away. Choose a 30-year climatology (1991–2020 or 1961–1990) or any single month from 1961 to the present. Toggle Mean ↔ ±1σ variability. Peel the stationary eddy off the zonal mean. Subtract one era's base state from another's to see the climate-change signal. Seed a Lagrangian parcel and watch it ride the monsoon anticyclone. Slice any cross-section; read off the Hadley cell strength or the EP-flux divergence or the moisture transport convergence.

It's for graduate students encountering the circulation for the first time, researchers sketching a hypothesis before opening a Jupyter notebook, educators pulling a figure for a slide deck, communicators explaining a weather-or-climate story, and anyone just curious what the jet stream actually looked like the week they were born.

It is the atmospheric sibling of TC-ATLAS, sharing its philosophy: free, open, and fast in the browser.

  • Grounded in the canon Every feature maps to a classical topic: Hadley / Ferrel / polar cells, jets, stationary Rossby waves, storm tracks, monsoons, the stratospheric circulation, Lorenz energy cycle, angular momentum.
  • Reanalysis-native ERA5 monthly means from 1961 to the present — eight sliding 30-year climatologies with their own ±1σ tiles, plus a per-year tree from 1991 onward — precomputed and served compressed (float16 + gzip) from Google Cloud Storage. No pip install, no Jupyter, no NetCDF download.
  • Decomposition, not just display Raw fields are the starting point. The payoff is the toggles — zonal-mean, eddy, anomaly, climate-change — that make the circulation's structure visible.
  • Globe-native A spinnable 3D globe (WebGL) with GPU-accelerated particle advection, true 3D Lagrangian trajectories, isentropic and pressure coordinates, and a heliocentric "view from space" mode. Built to scale to daily-data diagnostics next.
  • Open and adaptable Vanilla frontend, open pipeline, Copernicus-attributed data. Anyone can fork it, rewire it, or repurpose it — a classroom, a research group, a communication project.
