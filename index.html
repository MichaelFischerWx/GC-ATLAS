Why this tool

A foundation, made visible.

Graduate courses in atmospheric general circulation lean on static textbook diagrams. GC-ATLAS gives students a live reference — the real, observed base state they can spin, slice, and decompose — so the lectures build on intuition students already have.

The atmospheric general circulation is famously hard to teach. The concepts are three-dimensional, span the globe, evolve with the seasons, and can only be fully appreciated as decompositions — zonal mean versus eddy, stationary versus transient, rotational versus divergent.

GC-ATLAS is a pedagogical companion to the course: an always-on, interactive ERA5 climatology (1991–2020 by default) rendered as a windy-style globe, with live cross-sections, derived diagnostics, Lagrangian parcels, and budget viewers — the working tools of a general-circulation researcher, adapted for a classroom.

It is the atmospheric sibling of TC-ATLAS, sharing its philosophy: free, open, fast in the browser, built by the people who teach this material.

  • Built around the syllabus Every feature maps to a classical topic: Hadley/Ferrel/polar cells, jets, stationary Rossby waves, storm tracks, monsoons, the stratospheric circulation, Lorenz energy cycle, angular momentum.
  • Reanalysis-first ERA5 monthly means, 1991–2020 climatology, precomputed on cloud storage. Daily data where variance/transient diagnostics require it.
  • Decomposition, not just display Raw fields are the starting point. The pedagogical payoff is the toggles — zonal-mean, eddy, stationary, transient — that make the circulation's structure visible.
  • Globe-native A spinnable 3D globe (WebGL) with GPU-accelerated particle advection and true 3D Lagrangian trajectories — built to scale.
  • Open and extensible Vanilla frontend, FastAPI backend, open data. Instructors can fork it, rewire it, and author guided tours for their own courses.
